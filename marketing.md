## Coffee Trivia
- The word "coffee" comes from the Arabic "qahwa," which originally referred to wine. It was later adopted into Turkish as "kahve" and then into European languages.
- Coffee is the second most traded commodity in the world after oil.
- The first coffeehouse in the world opened in Constantinople (now Istanbul) in the 15th century. It quickly became a hub for intellectual discussion and business deals.
- The "coffee break" tradition is believed to have originated in the early 20th century as a way to boost worker productivity and morale. In 1952, the Pan-American Coffee Bureau launched a campaign promoting the idea of a "coffee break" to increase coffee consumption among office workers.

## What is Marketing?
When we talk about marketing, we often think of it as "advertising", "social media" or "we pay some Youtubers for promotion like Nord VPN", or "marketing is the same as sales". It's actually much more. So, what is marketing?
>Marketing is the process of discovering what customers need — and then letting them know you can meet that need.

Take a moment to read this definition 3 times in your head until you can recite it. (pause) Then reflect on what it means. What does it mean to "discover what customers need"? How do you find out what customers want? And how do you let them know you can meet that need? First, circle the word "process". Marketing is a process. A process means that it's not just one thing, or one time, but a series of steps and activities that you do over and over again. Second, circle all the verbs in that definition. The first verb is "discover". This means that you have to actively seek out to understand your customers' needs. You can't just assume you know what they want. You have to do research, ask questions, and listen to their feedback. The second verb is "let them know". This means that you have to communicate effectively with your customers. 

- **Marketing is not just Advertising:** Marketing is the entire process of understanding and reaching customers, while advertising is just one part of that process — the part where you create messages to promote your product. e.g., when you see a new trailer for a new movie, that's advertising. But the marketing has started long before that — from researching what kind of movies people want to see, to deciding on the movie's title and poster design, color themes, to choosing where to put the advertisements: on TV, on social media, on the box of McDonald's meals, or a preview in movie theaters.
- **Marketing is not just Sales:** Marketing starts before the customer walks in; sales happens once they're already there

## **No Marketing = No Success** 
![Top reasons startups fail](images/top-reasons-startups-fail.png)
Let's review the statistics we saw in the first chapter about startup failures. 42% of startups fail because their product doesn't solve a real problem and 14% of startups fail because of poor marketing. This combines to 56% of startups failing because they don't have a good marketing strategy. This is more than half of all startups. So, I will say marketing is not just important, it's actually the most important thing for a startup.

![A piece of cake](images/piece-of-cake.png)

We also saw that Americans alone spend over $100 billion on coffee every year. But does it mean that earning $5 out of that $100 billion cake is like a piece of cake? No. What the number doesn't tell you is this market is highly competitive. 

It tells you "what happened", but it doesn't tell you "how to make it happen". Knowing how comes from talking to your customers, understanding their needs, and creating products and experiences that they will love.

So one of your first tasks this week as a marketer is to talk to your potential customers. They can be your teachers, friends, parents, or even yourself. Ask them about their coffee habits, what they like and dislike about coffee, what they look for in a coffee stand at a school event, and what would make them want to buy from you. This is called market research. It's the process of gathering information about your customers and it's your direct experience, we call first-hand research. 

## We are not selling coffee
![public event](images/public-event.png)
When you talk to your customers, keep in mind that they are not just buying a cup of coffee. They are buying:
  - a moment of relaxation
  - a chance to socialize with friends
  - a quick energy boost
  - something to hold in their hand (and show off, why not?)
  - something that makes your parents proud of (club special, double win!)

Let's take Starbucks as an example.

Starbucks make a cup of coffee:
 - a moment of relaxation by providing a cozy seating area with soft music and free Wi-Fi (pause for a moment and slowly move on)
 - a chance to socialize with friends by creating a welcoming atmosphere and encouraging people to stay and chat
 - a quick energy boost by offering a wide variety of caffeinated drinks and snacks
 - something to hold in their hand (and show off) by designing visually appealing cups and offering seasonal drinks that people want to share on social media

Now imagine if Starbucks just focused on making good coffee, but didn't care about the experience, the atmosphere, the branding, and the social media presence. Would it be as successful as it is today? Probably not.

This is the power of marketing. It's not just ads, sales, or social media. It's product + experience.

> In one sentence: we are not selling coffee, we are selling happiness.


By understanding our customers, you serve better. You can create products and experiences that they will love — and that will keep them coming back.
- **Attract Customers:** You can have the best coffee in the world, but if no one knows about it, you won't have any customers. Marketing helps you get the word out and attract people to your stand.
- **Build a Brand:** A strong brand can create an emotional connection with customers, making them more likely to choose your coffee over others. Marketing helps you build and communicate your brand identity.
- **Drive Sales:** Ultimately, the goal of marketing is to drive sales. By effectively promoting your products and creating a strong brand, you can increase your sales and grow your club.

## Activity 1: Identify your customers (target market)

- Take a moment to think about who your customers will be. Are they adults or kids? What are their roles? Teachers? Students? Parents? What are their needs and pain points? What do they value when it comes to coffee? Write down your thoughts and share with the class.
- You don't have to serve everyone. Especially identify the people who are **not** your customers. These are the people who will never buy from you, no matter how good your coffee is. You have to accept that you can't serve everyone, and that's okay. What is not okay is to try to serve everyone and end up serving no one. For example, someone who is so picky about their coffee that they complain about the taste, the temperature, the size, the price. What do you do with this customer? Do you try to please them and end up making a bad experience for everyone else? Or do you politely explain that your coffee is not for everyone, and that's okay? This is the reality of marketing. You have to make choices about who you want to serve and who you don't want to serve.
In fact, it's better to focus on a specific group of customers who are most likely to buy from you. This is called your target market. This will help you create marketing messages especially for the people who are not buying from you. For example, someone who never drinks coffee come to your stand, you can politely explain that your coffee is not for everyone, and that's okay.

### 📚 Core Concepts

#### Target Market
Not everyone is your customer — find the group most likely to buy.

#### Market Research Methods
| Method | Best For | Pros & Cons |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Survey | Collecting large amounts of data | Fast and broad, but answers may stay surface-level |
| User Interview | Digging into real motivations | Rich insights, but time-intensive |
| Observation | Understanding actual behavior | Objective and real, but doesn't reveal the "why" |

#### Customer Persona
A semi-fictional profile of a "typical customer," including:
- Basic info (age, role/identity)
- Needs and pain points
- Buying habits and decision-making factors

### 🛠️ Classroom Activities

**Activity 3: Go Do Research (Week 3)**
- Students design a 5–8 question survey targeting classmates and teachers
- Collect at least 20 valid responses
- Key questions: purchase frequency, price willingness, top priorities (taste / price / convenience / social feel)

**Activity 4: Build the Persona (Week 4)**
- Consolidate survey data and visualize key findings with charts
- In groups, create 2 customer personas (e.g., "an 8th grader who wants to unwind after class" vs. "a teacher who needs a morning pick-me-up")
- Discussion: Do these two customers need different marketing approaches?

### 📝 Phase Deliverable
- **Customer Research Report** (data + key insights)
- **2 Customer Persona Cards** (visual format)

## Phase 3: Product & Brand (Weeks 5–6)

### 🎯 Learning Objectives
- Understand the difference between a brand and a product
- Learn the fundamentals of brand positioning
- Design a visual brand identity for the club's coffee stand

### 📚 Core Concepts

#### Brand Positioning
> What do you want customers to think of the moment they think of you?

Three elements of positioning:
- **Target Audience:** Who are we serving?
- **Differentiation:** How are we different from others?
- **Value Proposition:** What value do we deliver to customers?

#### Visual Identity System
- **Brand Name:** Simple, memorable, meaningful
- **Logo:** A visual symbol that communicates brand personality
- **Brand Colors:** Different colors carry different emotions (green = natural/healthy; brown = coffee/warmth)
- **Tagline:** One sentence that tells people who you are

#### Product Mix Strategy
- Core product vs. add-on products (e.g., cold brew + specialty syrup options)
- The marketing value of seasonal and limited-edition offerings

### 🛠️ Classroom Activities

**Activity 5: Brand Positioning Workshop (Week 5)**
- Groups each propose a brand positioning statement for the club
- Whole-class vote to select the strongest direction
- Discussion: How does brand positioning shape product names, packaging, and pricing?

**Activity 6: Design the Brand (Week 6)**
- Students sketch logo drafts (on paper or using Canva)
- Finalize a brand color palette (2–3 colors)
- Brainstorm 10 tagline options; narrow down to 1
- Invite the art teacher or a community mentor to provide feedback

### 📝 Phase Deliverable
- **Brand Book**, including:
  - Brand name and tagline
  - Logo (first draft)
  - Color palette
  - Brand story paragraph (3–5 sentences)

## Phase 4: Pricing & Channels (Weeks 7–8)

### 🎯 Learning Objectives
- Understand pricing strategies and the psychology behind them
- Analyze the pros and cons of different sales channels
- Develop a pricing sheet and channel strategy for the club

### 📚 Core Concepts

#### Pricing Strategies
| Strategy | Method | Best Used When |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| Cost-Plus Pricing | Cost × (1 + profit margin) | Ensuring you never sell at a loss |
| Competitive Pricing | Based on market/competitor prices | In a competitive market |
| Value-Based Pricing | Based on perceived customer value | When brand premium is high |
| Psychological Pricing | $4.90 vs. $5.00 | Boosting purchase intent |

#### Channel Strategy (Place)
- **In-person channels:** Fixed on-campus stand, pop-ups during breaks, school events
- **Pre-order channels:** Order forms (Google Forms / messaging apps)
- **Channel selection principle:** Go where your customers already are

### 🛠️ Classroom Activities

**Activity 7: Pricing Simulation (Week 7)**
- Present a cost breakdown for one cold brew (ingredients + packaging + labor time)
- Students calculate a price using each of the three main pricing strategies
- Whole-class debate: Which price is fairest? What would customers think?

**Activity 8: Channel Mapping (Week 8)**
- Identify all available sales channels for the club (between classes, lunch, school events)
- Draw a "customer purchase journey map": what steps does a customer take from first hearing about us to paying?
- Discussion: How do we reduce friction in the buying process (wait times, payment methods)?

### 📝 Phase Deliverable
- **Product Pricing Sheet** (with cost calculation notes)
- **Channel Strategy Document** (at least 2 channels + operational details)

## Phase 5: Digital Marketing & Social Media (Weeks 9–10)

### 🎯 Learning Objectives
- Understand the audiences and content styles of different social media platforms
- Master the core principles of content marketing
- Independently plan and publish real marketing content

### 📚 Core Concepts

#### Content Marketing
> Instead of pushing products directly, attract customers through genuinely valuable content.

**Content Types:**
- Educational ("Did you know the difference between cold brew and iced Americano?")
- Entertainment (behind-the-scenes of making a drink)
- Story-driven (a day in the life of a club member)
- Promotional (limited-time offer, new product launch)

**The Golden Ratio:** 80% valuable content + 20% promotional content

#### Social Media Operations Basics
- **Posting frequency:** Consistency matters more than volume
- **Visual consistency:** Use brand colors and a unified aesthetic
- **Engagement:** Reply to comments and messages to build community
- **Data tracking:** Likes, shares, comments, follower growth

#### Recommended Platforms for a Middle School Club
| Platform | Content Format | Advantage |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| Instagram | Photos / Reels | Visual showcase of products and behind-the-scenes |
| TikTok | Short video | Reaches peers organically |
| School bulletin board / group chat | Image + text | Direct reach to on-campus customers |

### 🛠️ Classroom Activities

**Activity 9: Content Planning (Week 9)**
- Analyze 3 successful food/beverage brand social media accounts
- Groups each take ownership of one content type (educational / entertainment / story / promotional)
- Create a **2-week content calendar** (posting times, themes, visual direction)

**Activity 10: Create & Publish (Week 10)**
- Each group creates and publishes at least 1 piece of content
- Return one week later to review performance: which post did best? Why?
- Discussion: How do we adjust our strategy based on what the data shows?

### 📝 Phase Deliverable
- **2-week social media content calendar**
- **At least 3 published posts** (screenshots + performance data)
- **Brief data analysis report** (what worked? what didn't?)

## Phase 6: Real-World Marketing Campaign (Weeks 11–12)

### 🎯 Learning Objectives
- Integrate knowledge from all five phases to plan and run a real marketing campaign
- Learn the complete campaign lifecycle: Goal → Strategy → Execution → Retrospective
- Build teamwork and project management skills

### 📚 Core Concepts

#### Campaign Planning Framework
1. **Goal Setting:** What do we want to achieve? (e.g., gain 20 new customers / double single-day sales)
2. **Target Audience:** Who is this campaign for?
3. **Core Message:** What do we want to communicate?
4. **Execution Strategy:** How do online and offline efforts work together?
5. **Budget:** How much are we spending? What's the expected return?
6. **Success Metrics:** How will we know if it worked?

#### Campaign Types (choose one)
- **New Product Launch:** Release a limited drink with a pre-launch teaser, launch day, and follow-up
- **Customer Appreciation Day:** Special offer for returning customers + feedback collection
- **Brand Story Campaign:** Share the club's founding story to build emotional connection
- **Collaboration Campaign:** Partner with another school club for cross-promotion

### 🛠️ Classroom Activities

**Activity 11: Campaign Planning (Week 11)**
- Whole class selects a campaign direction
- Groups divide responsibilities: Strategy team, Visual team, Social media team, On-site execution team
- Complete a full campaign brief (timeline, responsibilities, budget)
- Deliver a 5-minute pitch to "investors" (teachers/mentors), receive feedback, and revise

**Activity 12: Execute & Reflect (Week 12)**
- Run the marketing campaign
- Collect customer feedback after the event (short survey or verbal interviews)
- Hold a whole-class **retrospective meeting** (What Went Well? / What Can Be Improved?)
- Each student submits an individual reflection report

### 📝 Phase Deliverable
- **Campaign Brief** (full written document)
- **Campaign Execution Record** (photos / video / data)
- **Retrospective Report** (team version + individual version)

## Appendix: How This Curriculum Connects to Club Operations

The core principle of this curriculum is that **every marketing concept directly serves the club's real operations**:

- Customer personas → Guide product development and pricing decisions
- Brand book → Used for actual packaging, menus, and stand design
- Content calendar → Runs the club's real social media accounts
- Marketing campaign → Tests strategies in a real setting and generates real revenue

This approach ensures students don't just *learn* marketing — they actually *use* it.
