# Coffee Startup Club - Week 7: Financial Literacy and Business Management

In Week 5 we calculated the cost of a single drink. This week we zoom out and look at the whole business.

A business that makes a great product but loses track of its money will not survive. Financial literacy is not about being good at math. It's about understanding the story that numbers tell — and using that story to make better decisions.

## The Language of Business Finance

There are a few key terms every entrepreneur needs to know. Let's learn them using our coffee stand as the example.

**Revenue**
The total money you collect from customers. If you sell 50 drinks at $3.50 each:
> Revenue = 50 × $3.50 = $175.00

Revenue is not profit. It's just the money that comes in before expenses.

**Expenses**
Everything you spend to run the business. These fall into two categories:

- **Variable costs** — change depending on how much you sell (ingredients, cups, lids)
- **Fixed costs** — stay the same regardless of sales (equipment, setup fees, printed materials)

For our stand, most costs are variable, which is actually good — it means our costs go up only when our sales go up.

**Gross Profit**
> Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

From our Week 5 example: $175.00 − $88.50 (50 × $1.77 COGS) = **$86.50 gross profit**

**Net Profit**
After you subtract *all* expenses — including fixed costs — what's left is your net profit. This is the real bottom line.

**Break-Even Point**
The number of drinks you need to sell to cover all your costs before you start making profit.

> Break-Even = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price per drink − COGS per drink)

Example: If fixed costs are $30 (supplies, setup), price is $3.50, and COGS is $1.77:
> Break-Even = $30 ÷ ($3.50 − $1.77) = $30 ÷ $1.73 = **18 drinks**

After selling 18 drinks, every additional drink is pure profit. Knowing your break-even number is one of the most useful things you can know before a sales event.

## Tracking Your Business

You don't need fancy software. A simple spreadsheet — or even a notebook — will do.

Track these three things for every event:
1. **How many drinks sold** (by type)
2. **Total revenue collected**
3. **Total supplies used** (approximate ingredient cost)

After the event, calculate your gross profit. Over time, this data will show you which drinks are most profitable, which events are worth attending, and where you are wasting money.

## Making Business Decisions

Financial data is not just for accountants. It helps you make decisions:

- Should we raise prices? (Look at margin and customer sensitivity)
- Should we expand the menu? (Does it add meaningful revenue or just complexity?)
- Should we do more events? (Is each event profitable after all costs?)
- Where are we spending too much? (Which ingredient costs can be reduced?)

Every decision in a business has a financial consequence. Understanding the numbers means you make decisions with your eyes open.

## Part 2: Build Your Business Plan

Today each team will build a simple one-page business plan using everything learned so far.

Your business plan should include:

**1. Business Overview**
- Stand name and tagline
- What you sell (your menu)
- Who your customers are

**2. Pricing & Costs**
- Cost per drink for each menu item (from Week 5)
- Selling price for each item
- Gross margin per item

**3. Event Projections**
- How many drinks do you expect to sell?
- What is your revenue projection?
- What is your break-even number?
- What is your projected gross profit?

**4. Goals**
- What does success look like for your first event?

Present your plan to the group. Be ready to explain your pricing decisions and your projections.
