# Coffee Startup Club - Week 5: Product Development and Pricing

You have a brand. You can brew. Now we need to figure out what exactly we are selling — and how much to charge for it.

This week is about turning your drinks into a real product line with real prices. Not guesses. Not "whatever feels right." Real numbers based on real costs.

## What is a Product?

A product is more than the thing you make. It's the whole experience of buying it.

When a customer buys a coffee from you, they are buying:
- The drink itself
- The cup, lid, and any packaging
- The experience of being served
- The feeling of supporting something they believe in

That's your product. The drink is just the start.

## Building Your Menu

A good menu for a student coffee stand has a few key qualities:
- **Short:** 3–5 drinks maximum. Too many choices slow down the line and overwhelm customers.
- **Distinct:** Each item should feel different from the others, not just a variation of the same thing.
- **Executable:** You should be able to make every item quickly and consistently under pressure.

Start with the three foundation drinks from Week 3. You will expand in Week 10.

## Understanding Your Costs

Before you set a price, you need to know what it costs to make each drink.

**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** — the cost of the ingredients for one drink.

Let's work through an example:

| Ingredient | Amount used | Cost per unit | Cost per drink |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coffee grounds | 15g | $0.08/g | $1.20 |
| Milk | 100ml | $0.003/ml | $0.30 |
| Cup + lid | 1 set | $0.25 each | $0.25 |
| Stir stick | 1 | $0.02 each | $0.02 |
| **Total COGS** | | | **$1.77** |

That means it costs $1.77 to make one milk coffee. Now, what should you charge?

## Setting a Price

A price that is too low means you don't cover your costs. Too high and customers walk away.

**The formula:**

> Price = COGS + desired profit margin

A typical food business targets a **60–70% gross margin**. That means if your COGS is $1.77, your price should be around:

> $1.77 ÷ (1 - 0.65) = **$5.06**

For a school stand, that may be too high. Adjust based on your customer — middle school students and teachers have a different price tolerance than a downtown café customer. A price of **$3.00–$4.00** is often the right range for school events.

**Key rule:** Never price below your COGS. Even in a learning context, the math has to work.

## Profit, Revenue, and What They Mean

- **Revenue** = total money collected from sales (price × number of drinks sold)
- **COGS total** = cost of all ingredients used
- **Gross profit** = Revenue − COGS total
- **Gross margin %** = Gross profit ÷ Revenue × 100

Example: You sell 20 cups at $3.50 each.
- Revenue = $70.00
- COGS total = 20 × $1.77 = $35.40
- Gross profit = $70.00 − $35.40 = **$34.60**
- Gross margin = $34.60 ÷ $70.00 = **49%**

Not bad for a first event.

## Part 2: Hands-On Pricing Activity

Today each team will calculate the real cost of their drinks and set their menu prices.

**Step 1 — Ingredient inventory (10 min)**
Use the ingredient list and price sheet provided. Write down every ingredient in each of your three drinks and its cost per unit.

**Step 2 — COGS calculation (10 min)**
Calculate the cost per drink for each item on your menu. Use the table format above.

**Step 3 — Set your prices (5 min)**
Based on your COGS, set a price for each drink. Aim for at least 40% gross margin.

**Step 4 — Revenue projection (10 min)**
Estimate: if you sell 30 drinks at an event, what is your total revenue and gross profit? Is it worth it?

**Step 5 — Present your menu and pricing (5 min per team)**
Share your menu, prices, and projected profit with the group.
