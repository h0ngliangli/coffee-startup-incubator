# Coffee Startup Club - Week 6: Sales and Customer Service

You have a product. You have a price. Now someone has to actually sell it.

This is the week a lot of students feel nervous about. Talking to strangers. Asking for money. Handling a line of customers. It feels uncomfortable — until you do it a few times. Then it becomes one of the most satisfying parts of running a business.

## What is Sales?

Sales is not convincing people to buy something they don't want. That's manipulation — and it doesn't build lasting businesses.

Real sales is **helping someone find what they need and making it easy for them to say yes.**

When someone walks up to your stand, they are already interested. They walked over. Your job is to:
1. Make them feel welcome
2. Help them choose the right drink
3. Make the transaction smooth
4. Leave them feeling good about the experience

That's it. No pressure. No tricks.

## The Customer Journey at Your Stand

Think about every step a customer takes from the moment they notice your stand to the moment they walk away with a drink:

1. **They notice your stand** — Does it look inviting? Is the menu clear?
2. **They approach** — Do you make eye contact? Do you smile? Do you say hello?
3. **They look at the menu** — Is it easy to read? Are prices visible?
4. **They order** — Do you confirm the order clearly? Repeat it back to them?
5. **They wait** — Do you keep them updated? Is the wait time reasonable?
6. **They receive their drink** — Do you hand it to them properly? Do you say "enjoy" or "thank you"?
7. **They walk away** — Would they come back? Would they tell a friend?

Every one of those steps is a chance to make the experience better or worse.

## What Makes Great Customer Service

**Be present.** Put your phone down when a customer is at your stand. Give them your full attention.

**Be clear.** Speak up. Know your menu well enough to answer questions: "What's your most popular drink?" "Do you have anything without caffeine?" "How sweet is it?"

**Be honest.** If something is sold out, say so immediately. If a drink will take longer than usual, let them know. Customers forgive delays — they don't forgive surprises.

**Handle mistakes gracefully.** You will make a wrong drink. A cup will spill. A customer will be unhappy. Stay calm, apologize, fix it. How you handle a mistake leaves a stronger impression than when everything goes smoothly.

**Say thank you.** Every time. Even when you're busy.

## Handling Difficult Situations

**"This isn't what I ordered"**
Stay calm. Apologize without arguing. Ask what they ordered and make it right. Don't explain what went wrong — just fix it.

**"This tastes off / I don't like it"**
Apologize and offer to remake it or offer a refund. Don't get defensive about your recipe.

**"How long is the wait?"**
Give an honest estimate. If you're unsure, say "a few minutes" and then prioritize their order.

**Long line forming**
Acknowledge waiting customers with a smile or a quick "We'll be right with you!" It doesn't speed anything up, but it shows you see them.

## On-Shift Standards

When you are on shift, you represent the business — not just yourself. Customers form their impression of your brand in seconds. How you carry yourself matters as much as how good your coffee is.

**Focus**
- If a customer is at the stand or could approach at any moment, you are on duty.
- Save conversations with friends for after your shift or during a designated break.
- One person should always be watching the stand, even when it's slow.

**Phones**
- Phones stay in your pocket when customers are present.
- If you need to use your phone (timer, calculator), use it quickly and put it away.

**Breaks**
- Breaks are rotated so the stand is never left understaffed.
- Ask your team lead before stepping away — never just disappear.

**Team communication**
- Keep side conversations short and quiet when customers are nearby.
- If something goes wrong, solve it together calmly — don't argue in front of customers.

**Why this matters**
A customer who walks up and sees the team laughing with friends instead of noticing them will walk away. You may never know you lost that sale. Professionalism isn't about being stiff — it's about making every customer feel like they are your priority.

## Part 2: Role-Play Selling Scenarios

Today we practice before the real thing. It's always better to make your mistakes here than in front of real customers.

**Setup:**
One team runs the stand. The instructor and other students play customers — some friendly, some difficult.

**Scenario 1 — Smooth transaction**
A friendly customer wants a milk coffee. They are happy, easy to serve. Practice the full flow from greeting to handoff.

**Scenario 2 — Indecisive customer**
A customer can't decide between two drinks and keeps asking questions. Practice guiding them without rushing them.

**Scenario 3 — Wrong order**
You made the wrong drink. The customer is slightly annoyed. Practice the recovery.

**Scenario 4 — Busy rush**
Three customers arrive at once. Practice managing the queue and keeping everyone calm.

**Debrief:**
After each scenario, the group gives feedback: What went well? What would you do differently?
